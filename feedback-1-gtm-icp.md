# Feedback #1 — GTM & ICP
**Subspace.money · Product Teardown · May 2026**

---

## (a) Observed

Subspace's GTM is all over the place. The website homepage tries to be five things at once — shared subscriptions, gift cards, rentals, a bill-splitting tool, and a marketplace for local subscription providers. There is no clear hook or singular message for a first-time visitor. The headline reads *"Your subscription management platform"* but the app store description positions it as a savings + rental + gift card product. None of these positioning statements point to a specific kind of user.

The *"India's first subscription marketplace for local providers"* claim — which is actually a strong B2B GTM angle — is buried in footer-level copy and has zero dedicated section on the homepage. The sign-in options on web are only WhatsApp or phone number, which means there is no low-friction web-first onboarding for someone casually exploring the product.

---

## (b) Problem

There are two distinct problems here — one on the GTM side, one on the ICP side.

**On the GTM side:** Subspace is not leading with its strongest product. The shared subscription model — where someone joins a Netflix or Spotify plan with strangers and pays a fraction of the cost — is the most viral, most word-of-mouth-able feature they have. It is genuinely differentiated in the Indian market. But instead of going all-in on this with a focused GTM, the product is diluted across too many verticals. A user landing on the site cannot immediately understand why this is worth signing up for over just buying their own subscription directly.

**On the ICP side:** The product's real users — based on Play Store reviews — are overwhelmingly college students and young working professionals aged 18–28 who are price-sensitive and comfortable with group sharing. Reviews mention students signing up with college emails for extra deals, users sharing Netflix with strangers to save money, and working professionals splitting SaaS tools like Canva or Microsoft Office. But the app and website copy does not speak to this person directly. There is no "built for students" or "split your OTT bills with your hostel group" framing. The product treats everyone as the ICP, which means it converts nobody specifically.

### User evidence from Play Store reviews (April 2026)

> "would advise against shared subscription as you're dependent on admins" — points to the shared subscription being the core use case, and its biggest pain point.

> "An admin removed me from a group because I requested an OTP twice" — the shared subscription model is the stickiest product but also the most trust-broken one.

> "Extra deals for students who sign up with a college email" — student segment is clearly being targeted in-app, but not in any public GTM material.

> "had no troubles while buying individual memberships" vs "had problems with the shared subscription" — two different ICPs with completely different trust and satisfaction levels, both lumped into one product experience.

---

## (c) Ship Instead

Narrow the GTM to the shared subscription model exclusively, at least for the next growth phase. This is the product's biggest network-effect driver — every new group member makes the platform stickier for the admin, and every admin who builds a reliable group becomes a de facto micro-distributor for Subspace. Gift cards and rentals can stay in the app but should not compete for homepage real estate.

On the ICP front, lead with the college student and young professional segment explicitly. Something like *"Split Netflix, Spotify, and Canva with your circle — pay 20% of the cost"* as the primary homepage headline is concrete, benefit-forward, and speaks directly to an 18–26 year old who is tired of paying full price. The student email deal — which is already live in-app — should be the first thing on the landing page, not hidden inside the app.

The shared subscription model also has a genuine quality problem that is hurting the GTM — admins behaving badly and users losing money creates negative word-of-mouth that no amount of marketing spend can overcome. Fixing the trust layer inside the shared subscription (escrow, admin accountability scores, dispute resolution) is therefore not just a product fix — it is a GTM prerequisite. A focused GTM on a broken product loop will only accelerate churn. Nail the shared subscription experience first, then scale the marketing around it.
