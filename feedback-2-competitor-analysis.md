# Feedback #2 — Competitor Analysis
**Subspace.money · Product Teardown · May 2026**

---

## (a) Observed

When you look at the competitive landscape for the shared subscription model specifically — not bill splitting, not gift cards, just the core business of facilitating group plans for OTT and SaaS subscriptions — there is effectively no serious, active competitor in India right now.

Splitsub, a YC W21 company out of New Delhi, was the closest thing. It was literally described at the time as "Pinduoduo for online subscriptions in India." They raised $130K from Y Combinator in 2021 and have had no funding, no notable product updates, and no press coverage since. Their Mosaic score (a CB Insights health metric) is declining. They appear to have stalled.

The other names that show up in competitor lists — myPaisaa, MoneyClub, Finlok — are group savings and chit fund apps. They are not in the subscription sharing space at all. They solve a different problem for a different user.

On the global front, platforms like Spotify and Netflix have actively cracked down on informal password sharing since 2023, which means the behaviour that Subspace is building infrastructure for is being pushed off the big platforms and needs a trusted middleman more than ever.

---

## (b) Problem

The problem here is not that there are too many competitors. The problem is that Subspace does not seem to know it is sitting on an unclaimed market, and is not acting like it.

A company that recognises it has a wide-open category should be moving fast to become the default name people think of when they want to split a subscription. Instead, Subspace is spreading effort across gift cards, rentals, and a local subscription marketplace — verticals that each have much stronger, more funded competition (Amazon for gift cards, Zepto and Blinkit for rentals). In doing so, it is leaving its most defensible and most competitively empty market — shared subscription infrastructure — relatively under-invested.

The Netflix and Spotify password-sharing crackdowns since 2023 have made this more urgent, not less. Millions of Indian users who used to share accounts casually now need a structured, trusted platform to do it legally and safely. That is exactly the problem Subspace was built to solve. But because the product experience of shared subscriptions is currently broken — admin abuse, refund friction, no trust layer — Subspace cannot capitalise on this tailwind. Every person who gets burnt by a bad admin experience tells five people not to try it.

The window to own this category is real, but it is not permanent. If a well-funded player — a PhonePe, a Paytm, or even a funded startup with better execution — decides to enter the shared subscription space, Subspace's current product gaps become existential.

---

## (c) Ship Instead

Double down on shared subscriptions as the primary product and treat everything else as secondary. The goal right now should be to build a moat so deep in this one vertical that any new entrant looking at the market says "Subspace already owns this." That moat is built through three things: trust, scale, and brand recall.

**On trust** — fix the admin accountability problem before any new marketing spend. Introduce an escrow-style payment hold where a subscriber's money is only released to the admin after they successfully log in and confirm access. If any competitor enters this space, the first thing they will advertise is safety. Subspace should make that attack impossible by owning the trust narrative first.

**On scale** — run a focused college campus GTM. Tier-1 engineering colleges in particular have dense populations of exactly the right ICP: 18–24 year olds, sharing hostel rooms, already splitting Zomato orders and Uber rides, deeply price-sensitive on OTT. A campus ambassador programme where the top student in each college becomes the default admin for Netflix/Spotify group plans would create locked-in organic growth at near-zero CAC.

**On brand recall** — own the phrase "split your subscription" in India the way Splitwise owns "split your bill." Right now neither Subspace nor anyone else owns this phrase. It is an open goal. A single focused content and SEO push around this positioning, tied to a cleaner product experience, could define the category before any competitor even notices it exists.
